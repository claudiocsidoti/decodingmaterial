The luxury of sacrifice

Branding as the arbiter of meaningful friction in an AI-colonised market

From data collection and systematization to optimized production, to customer experience and service, AI is revolutionising the fashion and design industry across all sectors of the supply chain. But in an era of synthetic perfection, status is rather measured in sacrifice. Future clients are going to pay not for optimization, but for the right to consume the only prime matter that AI cannot replicate: human time.

AI-powered or AI-colonized?

Automation holds value by itself as a feature in a B2B context, where the sector of implementation is the customer. A B2C relationship, on the other hand, implies an exchange of roles: the product becomes the tool, the customer becomes the creator, and individual customers come into the picture.

Brands that want to survive must claim authority over AI. The reason?

When a product or service employs artificial intelligence to the point that the original value proposition is disproven by the very means of its delivery, the good itself is downgraded to the status of vessel for algorithm performance.

So the means become the end, the tool the offer, the helper the predator. It's not just a product, or a brand, eating up another. It's tech eating up any other industry by assimilation, disguised as service.

The danger of this cultural techno-parasitism is best shown by its effects on brand equity. If the AI craze is at its highest, why are customers leaving the conversation?

Customers cannot be automated

Rapid fatigue is the result of this semiotic mismatch between an unreliable form and an offer unrelated to expectations. If a client in want of distinction gets approached by a machine instead, they feel insulted, unworthy of human time, and leave a conversation where they are considered just a number.

The trust of customers is not earned by antagonizing increasingly sophisticated technology on its own ground as efficient executors, but by moving the competition onto what AI has no credibility doing: caring about the process, cultural legibility, negotiating the analog. By valuing what AI cannot be just as, if not more than, what it can do.

Branding antibodies to digest AI tools

In an AI-powered culture, businesses need more than a public persona that signals value and ignites aspirational choices. They also need an inner filter to govern their own tools, taming their previous role as products, and tying them to the value proposition they are now serving. Brand is called to fulfil this double role, informing both production and marketing strategies.

Branding is the new arbiter of what can be automated, and to which degree, and what instead requires meaningful friction.

The practical execution must adhere to quality standards, therefore it can be outsourced to machines. But the concept and the conversation with consumers—the ‘Why?’ and the communication of this ‘Why?’, from the concept down to the marketing and copywriting—must remain human-generated. AI must not be let alone to think or connect.

The solution: neo-craftsmanship

In 5 years’ time sacrifice, not perfection, will be the ultimate luxury. A ‘Why?’ worth sacrificing human time and effort for, is going to be the real product, and the physical object or service a vessel of that sacrifice.

A neo-craftsmanship model might entail a more personal relationship between consumers and individual artisans employed within a business, entertaining a 1-on-1 preferential exchange of taste and suggestions. AI would provide the dialogue infrastructure to an affiliation that accumulates values over time.
Scarcity packaged as care might take the form of timelines where customers can follow the creation of the product they’ve purchased, along with pictures of the in-between stages, materials and tools used.
AI can be used as a black-box engine to generate design options, but they are valuable only when they are rooted in a man-made concept with deep-running backstory. Human discernment must then secure brand authority, by selecting the best options in accordance with the original vision.

Ultimately, consumers purchase the experience of being especially catered to. Automation severs a business’ sensitivity to their needs, making them feel like a problem to be solved. Maybe the problem that AI wants to solve was the real value to foster all along: human lack is the space where things begin to matter.
